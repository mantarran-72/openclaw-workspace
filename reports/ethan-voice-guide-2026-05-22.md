# Ethan Voice Guide - First Pass

Source basis: read-only review of business sent mail from `ethan@aerospectinc.com`, focused on AeroSpect, drone imaging, vineyard operations, investor/partner follow-up, and data delivery. This guide intentionally does not store raw email bodies.

## Core Voice

Ethan writes like an operator-founder: practical, direct, technically credible, and relationship-aware. The tone is warm without being performative. He usually starts by acknowledging the other person's time or context, then moves quickly into concrete information, constraints, or next steps.

The voice is strongest when it connects technology to field reality: timing windows, sensor choices, calibration, acreage, seasonality, regulatory bottlenecks, budget pressure, and workflow adoption.

## Default Tone

- Clear and grounded.
- Polite, but not overly polished.
- Specific rather than promotional.
- Confident when discussing operational advantages.
- Candid about constraints, tradeoffs, and uncertainty.
- Relationship-first, especially in follow-ups and introductions.

## Common Moves

- Opens with a human acknowledgment: good to connect, thanks for the time, appreciate the intro, understandable constraint.
- Quickly names the practical point: cost, schedule, deployment timing, data quality, waiver advantage, workflow fit.
- Uses concrete evidence: Gallo interest, RIFFAI PoC, non-geographic BVLOS waiver, acreage/day, seasonal window, sensor/platform specifics.
- Frames technical differentiators as operational unlocks, not abstract features.
- Ends with a low-friction next step: happy to walk through it, let me know what would be useful, I can send the deck, happy to answer questions.

## Signature Themes

- High-frequency imagery matters because it shifts growers from reactive documentation to proactive decisions.
- Agriculture is not a generic drone services vertical; data quality depends on timing, calibration, sensor choice, crop context, and interpretation.
- The non-geographic BVLOS waiver is a timing and scalability advantage because it removes site-by-site waiver delays.
- Hardware alone is not the moat; the operating system is capture, calibration, processing, reporting, regulatory compliance, domain expertise, and workflow integration.
- Budget pressure is real, so offerings should be framed around seasonal windows, shared site coverage, and practical adoption paths.
- Aerial data should complement, not replace, ground truth and grower expertise.

## LinkedIn Voice Principles

For comments:

- Keep most comments to 2-5 sentences.
- Lead with the real-world implication, not generic praise.
- Add one specific insight from aerial imaging, vineyards, GIS, BVLOS, thermal, multispectral, or field operations.
- Avoid sounding like a vendor pitch unless the post directly invites business discussion.
- When appropriate, position AeroSpect as complementary: aerial context plus ground-based/local operational data.

For posts:

- Use a concrete field/business observation as the hook.
- Explain why it matters operationally.
- Add one technical or regulatory detail that shows credibility.
- Close with a practical takeaway or open question.
- Avoid hype-heavy language like "revolutionary", "game-changing" unless tied to a specific constraint being removed.

## Preferred Phrases And Constructions

- "The barrier is cost."
- "That is an easy on-ramp."
- "Happy to walk through..."
- "Let me know if any additional context would be useful."
- "The gap is not adoption, it is frequency, resolution, and actionability."
- "This becomes infrastructure, not a one-off service."
- "I know the industry is facing real budget pressure..."
- "There is real value there, even if it is not the core value proposition."

Use these sparingly. They should shape tone, not become canned catchphrases.

## Avoid

- Generic AI/business jargon: "unlocking transformation", "revolutionizing agriculture", "leveraging synergies".
- Overly polished corporate phrasing.
- Excessive exclamation points.
- Long abstract claims without field evidence.
- Overclaiming defensibility from drone hardware alone.
- Sounding like a sales rep when a peer/operator comment would be more credible.

## Example LinkedIn Comment Styles

Strategic partnership / adjacent technology:

> This is a useful direction. Ground-based sensing can capture vine-level detail that aerial systems are not always designed to see, while aerial imagery gives the block-level context: vigor patterns, canopy temperature, irrigation response, and where to focus field time. The real value is probably in connecting those two views into a workflow growers can actually use.

BVLOS / autonomous operations:

> The operational piece matters as much as the aircraft. In specialty crops, the challenge is not just flying more often, it is getting repeatable data in the right window, with consistent calibration, and turning it into decisions before the window has passed. BVLOS helps because it removes a major deployment bottleneck.

Investor / market framing:

> I think the adoption question in vineyards is often misunderstood. Growers have used aerial imagery for decades; the limitation has been frequency, resolution, and actionability. Monthly imagery tells you what already happened. Higher-frequency thermal and multispectral data can start to show issues while there is still time to respond.

Budget-aware customer comment:

> That cost pressure is real. For research and production teams, the model probably has to match the seasonality of the need: high-frequency data during the decision window, without forcing a year-round hardware commitment that is hard to justify.

## Working Rule For Future Drafts

Draft in Ethan's voice by default as:

1. Concrete observation.
2. Operational implication.
3. Technical/regulatory detail only if it improves credibility.
4. Practical next step, question, or grounded takeaway.

Keep the tone confident but not performative.
